\chapter{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This thesis asked to what extent a semantically aware, scene-level 3D Gaussian Splatting autoencoder can be made to scale from a few hundred scenes to several thousand heterogeneous scenes, and which design choices are responsible. The answer, stated plainly, is that it can be scaled well beyond the regime in which the problem was previously shown to be feasible, but not yet to the same reconstruction quality as the small-scale reference, and that the choices responsible are, in decreasing order of the impact we observed, the spatially local per-token encoder, the number of primitives sampled under frozen splats, the fixed-frame Hilbert serialization of the Gaussian set, and the gauge-invariant and permutation-invariant reconstruction objectives. The unifying reason these particular choices matter is the thesis of the work: the obstacles to scaling are, to first order, obstacles of identifiability and structure, and they are best addressed by making the reconstruction target identifiable and the latent compositional.

The sub-questions can now be answered directly. For \textbf{RQ1}, the cause of the failure to scale is principally the non-identifiability of the reconstruction target once the splats are frozen, rather than a shortage of model capacity, and convergence and generalization are restored by changes to the data pipeline (sampling and ordering), to the encoder (spatial locality), and to the objective (gauge invariance), not by enlarging the model. For \textbf{RQ2}, the ordering and the objective both matter and act on different gauges: the Hilbert order makes the slot-aligned target spatially stable and therefore learnable, though it is necessary rather than sufficient; the gauge-invariant covariance objective makes scale and the full covariance learnable while the raw quaternion remains gauge-locked; and a smaller sampling budget is easier precisely because it packs less within-region nuisance into each latent token. For \textbf{RQ3}, per-primitive contrastive supervision and a disentangled latent do add semantic clustering and a usable separation between coarse layout and fine geometry without degrading reconstruction, although the semantic separability we obtain is modest. For \textbf{RQ4}, the latent is designed for, and admits, a hierarchical two-part flow-matching generator and an inpainting completion model, both of which we implement and train on the Stage~1 representations that the Stage~1 results show can support them; the choice of which representations those are is itself decided by measured properties of the latents rather than assumed, and the only part not yet finished is the large-scale quantitative evaluation of the second stage, which is the principal qualification on the conclusion.

The contribution of the work is therefore twofold and should be read with its limits in view. As a diagnosis, the thesis reframes scene-level 3DGS autoencoding as a problem of identifiability, separates the gauge freedom into the set-ordering, orientation, and placement facets, and shows how each can be removed from the learning signal. As a recipe, it pairs an identifiable target (Hilbert serialization, an appropriate sampling budget, permutation-invariant and gauge-invariant objectives) with a compositional latent (a spatially local per-token encoder and an anchor-relative token-local decoder), and provides evidence that this is what allows the autoencoder to scale. The honesty of the conclusion is part of it: the second stage is built and trained but its full evaluation is still pending, and the gap between large-scale and small-scale reconstruction quality, which we read as a residual gauge floor rather than a capacity shortfall, is the open problem that the scaling effort surfaced but did not close. Carrying the second stage to a complete evaluation on a converged latent, and driving completion from genuinely partial captures, is the natural continuation of this work.
