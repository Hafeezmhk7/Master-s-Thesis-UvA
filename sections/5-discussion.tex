\chapter{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

This thesis set out to build a semantically aware, scalable, scene-level 3DGS autoencoder, and to understand what makes such an autoencoder scale. Read together, the experiments of \autoref{sec:results} support the organizing claim of the work: the choices that moved large-scale convergence were the ones that increased the \emph{identifiability} of the reconstruction target and the \emph{compositionality} of the latent, while the choices that only added capacity or conditioning did comparatively little. This section interprets that evidence, places it against the immediate predecessor, and is explicit about what the work does not yet show.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{What the Evidence Supports}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The central pattern across the ablations of \autoref{sec:res_identifiability} is consistent with the identifiability thesis rather than with a capacity explanation. Changing how the Gaussian set is ordered, how many primitives are sampled, how the encoder localizes, and how the objective treats gauge each had a first-order effect on whether the model converged at scale. By contrast, the conditioning and disentanglement variants of \autoref{sec:res_semantic}, which add structure to the latent without changing the identifiability of the target, helped the \emph{organization} of the latent but were not what unlocked scaling. We read this as evidence that the scaling barrier is, to first order, a property of the target and the encoder, not of model size.

It is worth being precise about the comparison with Can3Tok \citep{gao2025can3tok}. We do not claim to improve on Can3Tok in the setting in which it was validated. Can3Tok establishes feasibility on the order of a few hundred scenes whose splats are jointly optimized during sampling, and it remains the reference point for that regime. Our contribution is to move into a different and harder regime, namely several thousand heterogeneous scenes whose splats are pre-fitted and frozen, to diagnose why that regime is harder, and to show which changes make it tractable. The relationship is one of extension and diagnosis, not of a head-to-head win on a shared benchmark.

\section{The Scaling Problem and Its Partial Resolution}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Sampling as an identifiability lever.} The most counter-intuitive finding is that, with frozen splats, sampling fewer primitives per scene (on the order of $10$k) converges better than sampling more (on the order of $40$k), which is the opposite of the original setting. The interpretation we favour follows directly from the within-region placement gauge of \autoref{sec:method_overview}. The latent has a fixed number of tokens, so a larger sampling budget packs more primitives into each token's block (roughly $79$ per token at $40$k versus $20$ at $10$k for a $512$-token latent), and the additional primitives are exactly the within-block arrangement that is least identifiable. In the original pipeline the splats are still being optimized while they are sampled, so the fit can co-adapt the primitives to the tokenized target; with frozen splats no such adaptation is available, and the larger target is simply more nuisance for each token to encode. This reframes what looks like a hyperparameter, the number of sampled primitives, as a lever on target identifiability that behaves differently depending on whether the splats are frozen.

\paragraph{Ordering: necessary but not sufficient.} Replacing the opacity rank with a fixed-frame Hilbert serialization was, in our experience, the single ordering change that most improved large-scale convergence, and the advantage of the Hilbert curve over the Z-order curve is consistent with its better locality \citep{wu2024ptv3}. We are deliberately careful not to overstate this. Ordering made the slot-aligned target learnable and improved convergence, but it did not on its own bring large-scale reconstruction to the quality of the small-scale reference. It is a necessary ingredient that has to be combined with the sampling and locality changes, and the residual gap that remains after ordering is, we think, the clearest signpost to the unfinished part of the problem.

\paragraph{Locality, and a corrected claim about generalisation.} We initially expected the spatially local per-token encoder to be what closes the train-to-test generalisation gap, on the basis of the small-scale phase, where a global bottleneck on a few hundred scenes did overfit, with held-out error well above training error. The large-scale numbers corrected this for us, and we report the correction rather than the original expectation. At the scale of several thousand scenes, every configuration generalises with a full-to-chunk ratio near one (\autoref{tab:gen}), and the converged models show validation error close to training error, so overfitting is simply not the binding constraint once the data is large enough. What the local encoder does at scale is improve \emph{absolute} reconstruction: it roughly halves the position error over the global bottleneck (\autoref{tab:ladder}) and lowers the colour and orientation errors as well. The compositional reading still holds, namely that local Gaussian patches recur across scenes and an encoder that reads each token from a local window learns features that transfer, but the evidence for it at scale is the absolute error, not a closed gap. We think being precise about this matters: the locality change is well supported, but for a different reason than we first assumed.

\paragraph{Two routes to the same floor.} A second finding worth drawing out is that two different changes, the anchor-relative position decoding and the structured per-token latent, each reduce the position error by about an order of magnitude on their own, and that the error then settles at a floor of roughly four hundred and fifty regardless of which route is taken (\autoref{tab:ladder}). That two independent interventions land on the same value, and that the value does not improve as opacity and scale keep improving, is what we read as the signature of the placement gauge: the part of each primitive's position that is not determined by the rendered scene cannot be fit by a parameter-space objective, and both interventions remove the same recoverable part and leave the same irreducible remainder. This is the most direct evidence in the thesis for the identifiability claim, and it is also why we are cautious about chasing the position error lower with more capacity, since the floor looks like gauge rather than underfitting.

\paragraph{Gauge-invariant objectives and the rotation channel.} The covariance objective behaves exactly as the identifiability analysis predicts. Under a gauge-invariant covariance loss the scale and the full covariance of the Gaussians are learnable and do converge, while the raw element-wise quaternion error barely moves over training. This is not a failure of the covariance objective; it is direct evidence for the quaternion-gauge facet of \autoref{sec:method_overview}. The covariance $\Sigma = R\,\mathrm{diag}(s^2)\,R^\top$ is identified by the data and is reconstructed, but the quaternion that parameterizes its orientation is only identified up to the double cover, axis relabelling, and, near isotropy, almost any rotation, so the raw quaternion error measures gauge noise rather than reconstruction error. The permutation-invariant set objective addresses the complementary within-block assignment gauge, which is what produced the washed-colour and round-blob failure of a slot-aligned loss. We are explicit that orientation under the raw quaternion metric is a residual unsolved gauge: the covariance is learned, but the raw orientation channel is not, and a metric that is blind to the gauge is the only one under which the orientation result can be read correctly.

\section{Semantic and Layout Structure}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The per-primitive contrastive objective produced visible category clustering in the principal-component visualizations of \autoref{fig:pca} and a modest but real linear-probe separability in \autoref{tab:semquality}. The disentanglement objectives produced a genuine separation between a coarse layout subspace and the fine geometry: swapping the layout subspace across scenes disrupted geometry far less than a cross-scene baseline (\autoref{tab:disentangle}), and a deterministic layout code was the most stable under partial observation, which is precisely the property a completion model needs. Two qualifications matter. First, these results were established mainly in the small-scale regime and are being re-confirmed for the large-scale model, so they are reported as exploratory. Second, the linear-probe accuracy is modest, so the semantic structure is real but not strong. The contribution here is therefore narrow and honest: per-primitive semantics and a layout axis can be added to the latent at no measurable cost to reconstruction, and they provide the structure that a hierarchical generator and a completion model are designed to use, but we do not claim a strong semantic representation.

\section{Limitations}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

\paragraph{Validity and the status of the numbers.} Several of the central results are, at the time of writing, qualitative or based on a single large-scale run that is still training, and the ablation tables of \autoref{sec:results} are designed but not yet filled with final numbers. The small-scale disentanglement and semantic-quality figures are from the exploratory phase and require re-confirmation. The claims of this thesis are therefore well-motivated and directional, and the experimental design that would quantify them is in place, but the quantitative support is incomplete and should be read as such.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.} The pipeline has many moving parts: a specific canonical normalization, a global per-scene coordinate frame computed by combining chunks, a particular sampling and serialization, and a large set of configuration flags. Exact reproduction depends on all of these, and the appendix documents them, but the very sensitivity of convergence to the data preprocessing and the flag configuration is itself a reproducibility risk that we do not minimize.

\paragraph{Scalability.} We scaled to several thousand scenes on multi-GPU hardware. Scaling to the full SceneSplat-7K and beyond, and to a larger latent, is untested, and the cost is non-trivial; in particular, the variant of the per-primitive semantic head used in the large-scale run is heavy and trains slowly, and a lighter per-primitive head would be preferable at larger scale.

\paragraph{Generalizability.} Training and evaluation are confined to indoor scenes from a small number of capture sources. Whether the representation transfers to outdoor or object-centric 3DGS, or to a different label taxonomy, is open. Because the semantic labels are present only on one subset of the data, the semantic supervision is also uneven across the training mixture.

\paragraph{Reliability of the orientation channel.} The rotation gauge-lock means that, under the raw quaternion metric, orientation is not reliably reconstructed, even though the covariance is. For strongly anisotropic Gaussians the orientation matters to the rendered appearance, so the practical consequence should be checked by visual inspection of reconstructions rather than assumed to be benign.

\paragraph{The second stage is trained but not yet fully evaluated.} The most consequential limitation concerns scope. The second-stage generator and the completion model, the latter being the task named in the title, are implemented and trained on the selected representations, but their large-scale quantitative evaluation was still running as this was written, so the second-stage results are presented as a setup with reserved space rather than as finished measurements. The reason the project ran out of time at this point, rather than earlier, is the Stage~1 scaling problem, which absorbed most of the effort. We treat this openly as the principal shortfall: the design and training are in place and the comparison is set up, but the evidence for the second stage is not yet at the level of the Stage~1 evidence, and a reader should weight the two accordingly.

\paragraph{Ethics.} The datasets are indoor scans of homes and offices, which can contain privacy-sensitive content, and generative models of real spaces raise the usual provenance and dual-use concerns. The data used here comes from public research datasets, but any downstream deployment of a scene generator or completer should consider the consent and privacy of the people whose spaces were captured.

\section{Future Work}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------

The clearest next steps follow from the limitations. First, the orientation channel should be supervised only through the gauge-invariant covariance and a rendering term, with the element-wise quaternion loss removed entirely, so that the orientation has a learnable target rather than a gauge to fit. Second, and more ambitiously, moving from parameter-space to image-space supervision through the virtual-camera rendering loss that the pipeline already supports would make the objective intrinsically gauge-invariant and may be the cleanest resolution of the within-region placement gauge, at the cost of differentiable rendering during training. Third, the sampling strategy should move from opacity ranking to a density-uniform, farthest-point-style selection that equalizes the per-token load, and the trade-off between the number of sampled primitives and the latent capacity should be revisited jointly. Fourth, the second-stage generator and completion model, which are trained here, should be carried to a full quantitative evaluation on a converged Stage~1 latent, and completion should be driven from a genuinely partial capture rather than from masked tokens of a fully encoded scene, so that the harder and more realistic version of the task is measured; the partial-observation stability of the deterministic layout code suggests that the layout-conditioned design is the most promising starting point. Underlying all of these is the one open problem this thesis does not close: bringing large-scale reconstruction to the quality achieved at small scale, which the residual position-error floor after ordering, sampling, and locality changes shows is not yet solved, and which we read as a gauge floor rather than a capacity shortfall.
