\section{What the Results Support}
\label{sec:disc_support}

The clearest finding in this work is that imposing a canonical spatial order
on the Gaussian set is a prerequisite for scaling the autoencoder beyond a
few hundred scenes. Without ordering, the model cannot learn a stable
slot-to-position correspondence, and the validation loss stagnates regardless
of architecture or objective. This is not about the \emph{method} of ordering
in particular---even a rough spatially consistent ordering helps---but about
the \emph{presence} of any consistent order. The Hilbert curve was chosen over
Morton ordering and opacity ranking because its locality guarantee (consecutive
indices are always spatially adjacent) gives the smoothest, most learnable
slot-to-position target~\citep{wu2024ptv3}.

The second clear finding is that the local encoder closes the generalisation
gap. The gap between training and held-out error in R2 ($5$--$8\times$) is a
recognisable memorisation signature: the model has enough capacity to store
per-scene codes but these codes do not transfer. Switching to the windowed
encoder eliminates this almost entirely. The reason, we believe, is
compositional: a local flat-floor patch activates the same encoder features
across rooms, and a shared per-token MLP decoder learns a general
``patch-features $\to$ local Gaussians'' function rather than a per-scene
lookup table. This compositionality is exactly what Hilbert ordering was designed
to enable: it groups similar local patches together so the local encoder
sees consistent training signal.

The 300-scene results add a complementary picture: the latent is capable of
carrying semantic structure when explicitly encouraged. The disentanglement
experiments show that a cross-reconstruction loss and orthogonality penalty
genuinely separate layout and geometry representations (the swap test reduces
geometry disruption from 6.5\% to 0.08\%). The semantic feature visualisations
show clear category clustering. These findings motivate two design choices for
the Stage-2 generator: (a) the hierarchical $z_s$/$z_g$ split for generation,
and (b) the use of B1-style conditioning for completion, where stability
under partial observation is paramount (Stab.@40\% of 0.761 for B1 vs.\ 0.365
for the best Strategy-A variant).

\section{The Frozen-Splat Regime}
\label{sec:disc_frozen}

One observation that emerged from this project, and that has not been
explicitly discussed in prior work, is the difference between training on
\emph{jointly optimised} Gaussians (Can3Tok's setting) and training on
\emph{pre-fitted, frozen} Gaussians (our setting). In the former, the Gaussian
attributes are adapted during autoencoder training and the fitting process
learns to produce Gaussians that are easy for the autoencoder to tokenise. In
the latter, the autoencoder must cope with Gaussians whose within-region
arrangement was determined entirely by a rendering-quality optimiser that has
no knowledge of tokens.

The practical consequence is that the within-block structural ambiguity is
much larger with frozen splats. Two Gaussians in the same local region
are interchangeable for rendering purposes but not for the slot-aligned
reconstruction objective. The Sinkhorn set loss addresses this at the loss
level (by matching within blocks by optimal assignment), but the deeper fix
is to reduce the block size $g$ so there are fewer interchangeable Gaussians
per token. This is why we believe a smaller sampling budget is better in our
setting, and why the large-scale training with $N=40{,}000$ (from the Can3Tok
default) converges more slowly than with $N=10{,}000$.

This distinction matters for the community: any application of a 3DGS
autoencoder to pre-computed scene reconstructions (which is the most natural
deployment scenario) will encounter the frozen-splat regime, and the optimal
hyperparameters for that regime differ from those reported in Can3Tok.

\section{What the Reconstruction Objectives Contribute}
\label{sec:disc_objectives}

The combination of the Sinkhorn set loss and the Bures codiag orientation
loss was motivated by practical failures observed during development: the
colour of reconstructed scenes was washed out (all primitives converged to
mean grey) and the Gaussian shapes were round blobs rather than elongated
splats.

The set loss addresses the colour problem. Without it, the decoder must
predict which of $g$ primitives in a block gets which colour, but the
``correct'' assignment is determined by the opacity-ranking-based fitting
which has no semantic meaning for the decoder. The Sinkhorn matching lets
the decoder produce any permutation of colours within a block and be graded
by the best assignment, which removes the arbitrary permutation from the
gradient.

The covariance loss addresses the orientation problem. Measuring orientation
through the covariance matrix rather than the quaternion means the metric
is parameterisation-invariant: two quaternions that represent the same rotation
(under sign flip or axis relabelling) score identically. The Bures
co-diagonalisation~\citep{villani2009ot} is numerically stable because the
square root $\vsigma^{1/2} = R\,\mathrm{diag}(|s|)\,R^\top$ is available
in closed form.

Both objectives are complementary and were kept throughout the large-scale
runs. Their relative contribution to the improvement over a pure element-wise
loss can be measured by ablating them out of R1, which is straightforward
to do but was not part of the main ablation plan.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:disc_limitations}

\paragraph{Incomplete large-scale results.}
The most significant limitation is that the large-scale training was still
running at the time of writing. The quantitative tables in
Chapter~\ref{chap:results} carry pending markers, and the conclusions about
the relative performance of R1 vs.\ R2 are based on partially-trained
checkpoints and qualitative inspection of reconstruction PLYs. The
final numbers may refine the picture.

\paragraph{Rotation accuracy.}
Even with the Bures codiag loss, the raw quaternion error metric is difficult
to interpret because two quaternions that represent the same rotation
are scored as different. The covariance error is the more meaningful metric
for orientation quality, and it converges. Rendering quality (PSNR, SSIM)
from the final checkpoint will provide the most direct measure of whether
orientation is correct.

\paragraph{Semantic supervision at scale.}
The per-primitive InfoNCE and the disentanglement objectives were only run at
300 scenes, not in the main large-scale experiments. Whether they provide
the same benefit at 6,000+ scenes is an open question. Run~R3 (planned,
with disentanglement at scale) will provide the first answer.

\paragraph{Scene completion not evaluated.}
The original project title includes ``scene completion'', which requires
the Stage-2 inpainting pipeline. This pipeline is designed and the
Stage-2 training is planned, but scene completion has not yet been evaluated.
This is the principal gap between what was proposed and what has been delivered,
and it is the primary direction for future work.

\paragraph{Coverage breadth.}
Training and evaluation are confined to indoor scenes. Whether the
representation transfers to outdoor, object-centric, or very large-scale
scenes is untested.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:disc_future}

The most immediate next step is to complete the large-scale training runs,
evaluate the best checkpoint with image-space metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS),
and then train the Stage-2 generator and evaluate scene completion at varying
partial-scan coverage fractions.

Longer-term, several directions are worth exploring with a view to a future
publication. First, the frozen-splat sampling insight suggests a systematic
study of the optimal block size $g$ as a function of scene complexity and
splat quality. Second, the within-block placement ambiguity could be fully
eliminated by replacing individual Gaussian targets with a density-based
target (e.g., comparing predicted and target occupancy rather than
per-Gaussian positions), removing the need for the Sinkhorn matching
altogether. Third, the semantic objectives could be extended to the large-scale
setting using SceneSplat++ labels~\citep{ma2025scenesplatpp} and multi-task
learning to jointly train reconstruction and semantic feature quality.
Finally, bringing scene completion to evaluation and benchmarking against
existing explicit scene completion methods would provide a strong narrative
for a conference submission.
