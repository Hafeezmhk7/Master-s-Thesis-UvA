\chapter{Methodology}
\label{chap:method}

This chapter describes everything that is actually implemented and trained.
We begin with the overall framework and the problem setup
(\S\ref{sec:method_setup}), then cover the data pipeline
(\S\ref{sec:method_data}), the encoder (\S\ref{sec:method_encoder}),
the reconstruction objectives (\S\ref{sec:method_objectives}),
the decoder improvements (\S\ref{sec:method_decoder}), the local
encoder that is the key scaling change (\S\ref{sec:method_local}),
the small-scale semantic experiments that motivated the design
(\S\ref{sec:method_semantic}), and finally the Stage-2 design
(\S\ref{sec:method_stage2}). A clear figure overview is provided
at the start; all equations are numbered and all architectural
choices are explicitly justified.

\section{Framework and Problem Setup}
\label{sec:method_setup}

\paragraph{Scene representation.}
A scene is a set $\cG = \{g_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ of $N$ Gaussian primitives, each
a 14-dimensional vector:
\begin{equation}
  g_i = (\vmu_i,\; c_i,\; \alpha_i,\; s_i,\; q_i)
  \in \bR^3 \times \bR^3 \times (0,1) \times \bR^3_+ \times \bS^3.
  \label{eq:prim}
\end{equation}
The anisotropic covariance of each primitive is
\begin{equation}
  \vsigma_i = R(q_i)\,\mathrm{diag}(s_i^2)\,R(q_i)^\top,
  \label{eq:cov}
\end{equation}
where $R(q)$ converts the unit quaternion $q$ to a rotation matrix.

\paragraph{Autoencoder objective.}
We train a variational autoencoder (VAE)~\citep{kingma2014vae} with encoder
$E_\phi$ and decoder $D_\theta$. The encoder outputs the mean and log-variance
of a diagonal Gaussian posterior $q_\phi(Z|\cG)$ over a token-structured
latent $Z\in\bR^{T\times d}$ ($T=512$ tokens, $d=32$ per token). The latent
is sampled by the reparameterisation trick and the training objective is the
evidence lower bound:
\begin{equation}
  \cL = \cL_{\text{recon}}(\hat{\cG}, \cG)
      + \beta\,D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(q_\phi(Z|\cG) \;\|\;
        \mathcal{N}(0,I)\bigr),
  \label{eq:vae}
\end{equation}
with $\beta = 10^{-5}$. The small $\beta$ keeps the latent highly
informative for reconstruction while maintaining enough regularity for the
second-stage generator.

\section{Data Pipeline}
\label{sec:method_data}

\subsection{Dataset and Normalisation}
\label{sec:method_dataset}

We train on SceneSplat-7K~\citep{li2025scenesplat}, a collection of 7,916
indoor 3DGS scenes reconstructed from ScanNet~\citep{dai2017scannet},
Matterport3D, Replica, ARKitScenes, ScanNet++, 3RScan, and Hypersim.
Each scene carries per-Gaussian ScanNet-72 category labels, which we use
in the small-scale semantic experiments (\S\ref{sec:method_semantic}) but
not in the main large-scale training runs.

The main training set consists of 3,800 chunked interior scenes preprocessed
from the ScanNet/Matterport source at $8\times8$ tile stride with
$6\times6$ overlap. Three additional sources (800 full interior scenes,
1,290 ARKitScenes, 856 ScanNet++ scenes) are added with semantic labels
disabled to avoid cross-dataset taxonomy mismatch, giving approximately
6,746 training scenes total. Validation uses 100 held-out full scenes and
88 disjoint held-out chunks.

Each scene is normalised into a canonical sphere of radius 10\,m. For
chunked scenes, a global coordinate frame is precomputed from the union of
all chunks belonging to the same room (stored in \texttt{norm\_factor.npy}),
so every chunk shares a consistent spatial frame. Full scenes are normalised
per-scene. This normalisation is essential for the Hilbert ordering
(\S\ref{sec:method_hilbert}) to be consistent across scenes.

\subsection{The Sampling Question}
\label{sec:method_sampling}

A scene typically contains far more Gaussians than can be encoded. We select
a fixed budget of $N$ Gaussians per scene by sorting all primitives by
opacity in descending order and retaining the top $N$. This is the
sampling strategy from Can3Tok~\citep{gao2025can3tok}.

An important subtlety distinguishes our setup from Can3Tok: in Can3Tok the
Gaussian attributes are \emph{jointly optimised} alongside the autoencoder
training, which means the fitting process can co-adapt the primitive placement
to the tokenisation. In our setting the splats are \emph{pre-fitted and frozen};
the autoencoder sees a fixed set of Gaussians whose within-region arrangement
was determined entirely by a rendering-oriented optimiser with no knowledge
of the tokenisation structure.

The practical consequence is that each latent token must decode
$g = \lceil N/T \rceil$ primitives. At $N=40{,}000$ and $T=512$,
$g \approx 79$~Gaussians per token; at $N=10{,}000$, $g \approx 20$.
The decoder has $d=32$ numbers per token to account for $g \times 14$
output values, giving compression ratios of roughly $32\,/\,(79{\times}14)
\approx 0.029$ versus $32\,/\,(20{\times}14) \approx 0.114$.
The larger the block size, the more structural ambiguity is packed into each
token, and the harder the task. We investigate this empirically in
\S\ref{sec:results_sampling}.

\subsection{Hilbert Serialisation}
\label{sec:method_hilbert}

After sampling, we reorder the $N$ selected Gaussians along a Hilbert
space-filling curve computed in the fixed canonical frame of radius 10\,m.
Concretely, each Gaussian position is mapped to an integer grid in
$[0, 2^{10})^3$ by normalisation, then encoded to a 1D scalar using the
Hilbert encoding of Skilling~\citeyearpar{skilling2004hilbert}, and the
Gaussians are sorted by this scalar.

The key property of the Hilbert curve is \emph{locality}: consecutive indices
are always spatially adjacent (each step moves by exactly one cell in the
quantised grid), whereas the Z-order (Morton) curve can jump across the
volume when a high-order bit flips. This locality matters for the
reconstruction loss because it means slot $i$ maps to a consistent,
predictable spatial neighbourhood across all scenes. Point Transformer
V3~\citep{wu2024ptv3} demonstrates the same advantage for point cloud
processing tasks. We fix the frame (radius 10\,m, same as the normalisation)
so the ordering is scene-agnostic: the curve traverses the same absolute
spatial cells in the same order for every scene, giving a stable
slot-to-region correspondence that generalises.

After sorting, contiguous blocks of $g$ Gaussians correspond to compact
local clusters. This is the same partition used by the decoder tokens:
decoder token $k$ reconstructs Gaussians from block $k$, and the scaffold
anchor for token $k$ is the centroid of that block.

\section{Encoder}
\label{sec:method_encoder}

The encoder architecture is inherited from Can3Tok~\citep{gao2025can3tok}
and Michelangelo~\citep{zhao2023michelangelo}. We describe it here for
completeness and to establish the notation used when we modify it in
\S\ref{sec:method_local}.

\paragraph{Input features.}
Each of the $N$ Gaussians contributes an 18-dimensional feature:
voxel-centre coordinates (3 dims, FNV hash of a $40^3$ grid for coarse
locality), voxel ID (1 dim), normalised position (3 dims), colour (3 dims),
opacity (1 dim), scale (3 dims), quaternion (4 dims).
These are lifted to two Fourier encodings~\citep{tancik2020fourier}---of
the voxel centre and the position, 8 frequencies each---and the raw
geometric parameters are appended, giving an 110-dimensional input per
Gaussian that is projected to model width $w=384$ by a linear layer.

\paragraph{Cross-attention bottleneck.}
A set of $K+1$ learned query tokens cross-attends to the projected Gaussian
features. Query 0 produces the \emph{shape embedding}
$e \in \bR^{384}$ (a global scene summary). Queries $1\ldots K$ with
$K=256$ produce the per-region geometry tokens. Six self-attention layers
then mix the query tokens. The output is a $257 \times 384$ token sequence
that is permutation-invariant with respect to the input Gaussians.

\paragraph{VAE bottleneck.}
A per-token linear projection produces mean $\mu$ and log-variance
$\log\sigma^2$ for each of the $512$ latent tokens (the geometry queries
are remapped to 512 via a linear layer). The latent $Z \in \bR^{512 \times 32}$
is obtained by reparameterisation.

\section{Reconstruction Objectives}
\label{sec:method_objectives}

The reconstruction term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:vae} combines three components.
All are active in every large-scale run.

\subsection{Slot-Aligned Loss with Hilbert Order}
\label{sec:method_slotloss}

With the Gaussian set Hilbert-ordered, the element-wise residual
\begin{equation}
  \cL_{\text{slot}} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \|\hat{g}_i - g_i\|_2
  \label{eq:slot}
\end{equation}
is computed between the $i$-th decoded primitive and the $i$-th target
primitive. Without ordering, this comparison is arbitrary. With Hilbert
ordering, slot $i$ corresponds to a spatially stable location and the loss
has a well-defined gradient.

Colour is decomposed into a per-scene mean $\bar{c}$ (predicted by the
decoder from the shape embedding) and a per-Gaussian residual
$r_i = c_i - \bar{c}$. This separates the global colour palette, which is
easy to predict, from fine local colour variation, which is harder. The
decoder is trained to predict both:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{c}_i = \underbrace{\hat{\bar{c}}}_{\text{from shape emb.}}
            + \underbrace{\hat{r}_i}_{\text{from latent token}}.
  \label{eq:color_residual}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Sinkhorn Set Loss}
\label{sec:method_setloss}

The Hilbert order fixes the \emph{between}-block assignment but not the
\emph{within}-block assignment: inside each block of $g$ Gaussians, which
predicted primitive should match which target primitive is still ambiguous.
A slot-aligned loss inside a block forces the decoder to emit a fixed
permutation, but that permutation is typically arbitrary (it depends on the
opacity ranking of the original fit). This leads to washed-out colours and
round, mean-shaped Gaussians.

We resolve this by matching predicted and target primitives \emph{within
each block} using entropic optimal transport. Given the $g \times g$ cost
matrix $C_{ij} = \|f_i - f_j\|_2^2$ where $f_i$ is a weighted feature
vector for primitive $i$ (concatenating position, colour, opacity, and a
covariance term), the soft assignment is:
\begin{equation}
  P^* = \arg\min_{P \in \mathcal{U}(1/g, 1/g)}
        \langle P, C \rangle + \varepsilon H(P),
  \label{eq:sinkhorn}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{U}$ is the transport polytope, $H(P) = -\sum_{ij} P_{ij}\log P_{ij}$
is the entropy of the assignment, and $\varepsilon = 0.05$ is the
regularisation. The assignment is computed by Sinkhorn
iteration~\citep{cuturi2013sinkhorn} (50 iterations), then used to gather
matched target primitives. Gradients flow only through the prediction, not
through the matching (following~\citet{carion2020detr}). The identity assignment
recovers Eq.~\eqref{eq:slot} exactly; the OT matching can only reduce the loss.

\subsection{Covariance Orientation Loss}
\label{sec:method_covloss}

For orientation and scale, a naive element-wise loss on the quaternion $q$
is unreliable because the covariance matrix $\vsigma = R(q)\,\mathrm{diag}(s^2)\,R(q)^\top$
is invariant to various reparameterisations of $q$ and $s$. We instead
compare the reconstructed and target covariances directly, using the
co-diagonalisation Bures metric:
\begin{equation}
  d_{\text{cd}}^2(\vsigma_p, \vsigma_t) =
    \left\|\vsigma_p^{1/2} - \vsigma_t^{1/2}\right\|_F^2,
  \label{eq:bures}
\end{equation}
where $\vsigma^{1/2} = R\,\mathrm{diag}(|s|)\,R^\top$ is the exact
symmetric positive-semidefinite square root (available in closed form,
no iterative solver needed). This metric, which equals the exact
2-Wasserstein shape distance when the two covariances
commute~\citep{villani2009ot}, measures the actual similarity of the
Gaussian shapes regardless of how they are parameterised. The total
reconstruction loss is:
\begin{equation}
  \cL_{\text{recon}} =
    \cL_{\text{slot}} +
    w_{\text{cov}}\,\frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{N} d_{\text{cd}}^2(\vsigma_i^p, \vsigma_i^t).
  \label{eq:recon_total}
\end{equation}
with shape weight $w_\text{cov} = 5.0$.

\section{Decoder Improvements}
\label{sec:method_decoder}

\subsection{Anchor-Relative Position Decoding}
\label{sec:method_anchor}

In the baseline Can3Tok decoder, the flat MLP predicts Gaussian positions
as absolute coordinates in the normalised canonical frame. This requires the
decoder to learn the absolute position of every primitive in every scene---an
unnecessarily hard problem. Inspired by Scaffold-GS~\citep{lu2024scaffold}
and GaussianCube~\citep{zhang2024gaussiancube}, we decompose the prediction:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{\vmu}_i = a_{\tau(i)} + \rho \cdot \tanh(o_i),
  \label{eq:anchor}
\end{equation}
where $a_{\tau(i)}$ is the spatial anchor for decoder token $\tau(i)$
(the centroid of that token's Hilbert block, predicted from the decoder
hidden state), $o_i$ is the raw offset predicted per-Gaussian, and
$\rho > 0$ is a global learnable offset scale (initialised at 2.0). The
$\tanh$ bounding ensures the offset is a genuinely local displacement: the
decoder is asked for ``which block centre, plus a small local correction''
rather than an absolute global coordinate.

The per-token anchors $\{a_k\}$ are computed during data preprocessing from
the actual Hilbert-block centroids of the input scene (stored alongside the
features), making the decomposition exact at training time. The scaffold
mode is set to \texttt{hilbert\_block} to ensure anchor~$k$ corresponds
exactly to block~$k$, which is the same partition the decoder token~$k$
will decode.

\subsection{Decoder Fourier Positional Embedding}
\label{sec:method_fourierpe}

We add a Fourier positional embedding to the decoder tokens before the
transformer. Each of the $T=512$ tokens is assigned a coordinate on an
$8\times8\times8$ voxel scaffold grid covering the canonical frame. The
Fourier encoding of these coordinates (8 frequencies, projected to width 384)
is added to the post-KL token embeddings. This gives the decoder a notion
of \emph{which spatial region} each token corresponds to, making the
position prediction task easier.

\subsection{Token-Local Decoder (R1 only)}
\label{sec:method_tld}

The inherited flat decoder passes the concatenated token representation
(shape $512 \times 384$) through a $1024$-dimensional hidden layer before
predicting all $N \times 14$ Gaussian attributes at once. The $1024$-d
bottleneck is a severe compression: $196{,}608$ input values pass through
1,024 hidden units, a ratio of $\approx192:1$. This bottleneck must encode
scene-specific information that cannot generalise, making memorisation the
dominant strategy at large scale.

In Run~R1 we replace this with a \emph{token-local decoder}: a shared
MLP applied independently to each of the $T = 512$ decoder tokens,
predicting the $g \approx 20$ Gaussians belonging to that token's block.
\begin{equation}
  \hat{g}_{[k]} =
    \mathrm{MLP}_\theta\!\left(h_k\right),
    \quad k = 1,\ldots,T,
  \label{eq:tld}
\end{equation}
where $h_k \in \bR^{384}$ is the post-transformer hidden state of token $k$,
$\hat{g}_{[k]} \in \bR^{g \times 14}$ is the block of decoded Gaussians,
and the MLP weights are shared across all tokens. Because the MLP is
applied per-token rather than globally, there is no cross-scene bottleneck.
The parameter count drops from $\approx 777$M (flat decoder) to $\approx 2$M
(token-local MLP), while the effective representation bandwidth per token
increases from $1024/512 = 2$ to $384$ dimensions.

\section{Spatially Local Encoder (R1 only)}
\label{sec:method_local}

With the globally-mixed bottleneck encoder, every latent token is a function
of the entire scene. This is not a problem at 300 scenes, where the model
can memorise individual scenes. At thousands of scenes, two global encoder
tokens representing the same physical corner of two different rooms are
computed from entirely different global contexts, giving them no structural
similarity. The model is implicitly asked to generalise by learning one
global ``scene context $\to$ token'' mapping per token position, which at
scale it cannot do.

We replace the six global self-attention layers after the cross-attention
bottleneck with \emph{windowed cross-attention}: geometry query $k$ attends
only to the Gaussians in blocks $k-\omega$ to $k+\omega$ (window half-width
$\omega = 1$), rather than to all $N$ input Gaussians. The global query~0
still attends to all Gaussians for the scene-level summary. No global
self-attention runs inside the encoder; cross-token interaction is handled
entirely by the decoder transformer.

This gives an end-to-end spatial alignment: encoder block $k$ processes
Gaussians from blocks $k-1$ to $k+1$ $\to$ latent token $k$ encodes that
region $\to$ decoder anchor $k$ is the centroid of block $k$ $\to$ the
token-local MLP decodes those Gaussians. A flat floor patch in one room
activates the same local encoder features as a flat floor in a different
room, because both are processed by the same small-window cross-attention
over similar local geometry. This compositional structure is what enables
generalisation at scale.

\section{Small-Scale Semantic Experiments}
\label{sec:method_semantic}

Before committing to the large-scale training strategy, we conducted
exploratory experiments at 300 scenes to understand what kinds of latent
structure are feasible and to motivate the design choices for the Stage-2
generator.

\subsection{Latent Disentanglement Variants}
\label{sec:method_disent}

We tested whether the latent could be split into a \emph{semantic
subspace} $z_s$ (16 tokens, encoding coarse scene layout and category
distribution) and a \emph{geometry subspace} $z_g$ (496 tokens, encoding
fine geometric detail). Three conditioning approaches were explored:

\begin{description}
  \item[Strategy~C (baseline)] Undifferentiated latent; all 512 tokens
    are geometry tokens with no semantic split.
  \item[Strategy~A (disentangled)] The shape embedding is projected to $z_s$
    via a separate mean/variance projection; $z_g$ comes from the geometry
    queries. Auxiliary prediction heads on $z_s$ predict mean colour,
    label distribution, and per-category centroids. Cross-reconstruction and
    orthogonality losses encourage independence between $z_s$ and $z_g$.
  \item[Strategy~B1 (cross-attention conditioning)] A deterministic
    \texttt{Layout16Projector} maps the shape embedding to a layout code
    $z_{\text{lay}} \in \bR^{16 \times 32}$ without VAE sampling. $Z$ contains
    512 pure geometry tokens; $z_{\text{lay}}$ conditions every decoder layer as
    keys and values in cross-attention.
\end{description}

\subsection{Per-Primitive Contrastive Objective}
\label{sec:method_infonce}

We also tested a per-primitive InfoNCE objective. A projection head maps each
decoded Gaussian to a normalised feature $f_i \in \bR^{32}$. The loss pulls
together Gaussians sharing a ScanNet-72 category label:
\begin{equation}
  \cL_{\text{NCE}} = -\frac{1}{M}\sum_{i=1}^{M}
    \log \frac{
      \exp\!\left(f_i^\top p_{y_i}/\tau\right)
    }{
      \sum_{k=1}^{K} \exp\!\left(f_i^\top p_k/\tau\right)
    },
  \label{eq:infonce}
\end{equation}
where $p_k$ is the mean L2-normalised feature for category $k$ across the
current batch, $y_i$ is the category label of primitive $i$, $\tau=0.10$
is the temperature, and $M$ is the number of labelled primitives in the
batch. We tested three variants:
\begin{description}
  \item[pgNCE] The projection head operates on decoder hidden states and
    produces per-Gaussian features. Gradient flows to the latent indirectly
    through the decoder.
  \item[PoolNCE] A pooling head operates directly on the $z_s$ tokens,
    providing a direct gradient. Mirrors the architecture of the decoder
    InfoNCE at $[B,1024]$ hidden dimension.
  \item[Pool+pgNCE] Both objectives active simultaneously.
\end{description}
These semantic experiments were conducted at 300 scenes using the
SceneSplat-7K chunks. The results (\S\ref{sec:results_semantic}) motivated
the Stage-2 design and confirm that semantic structure is achievable,
though these objectives are not active in the current large-scale runs.

\section{Stage-2 Generative Model Design}
\label{sec:method_stage2}

The second stage trains a flow-matching model to generate the Stage-1 latent
$Z \in \bR^{512 \times 32}$ from Gaussian noise. We adopt the linear
interpolation path (rectified flow~\citep{liu2023rectified,lipman2023flowmatching}):
$Z_t = t\,Z + (1-t)\,\epsilon$, $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,I)$, with a
velocity model $v_\theta(Z_t, t)$ trained to minimise
$\mathbb{E}\left[\|v_\theta(Z_t,t) - (Z-\epsilon)\|_2^2\right]$.
The backbone is a Diffusion Transformer
(DiT)~\citep{peebles2023dit,ma2024sit} with adaLN-Zero timestep conditioning.

\paragraph{Hierarchical generation.}
The latent has two semantically distinct parts:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $z_s = Z[:, 0:16, :]$ --- 16 tokens encoding coarse scene layout
    (learned by Strategy-A disentanglement at small scale).
  \item $z_g = Z[:, 16:, :]$ --- 496 tokens encoding detailed geometry.
\end{itemize}
We use a two-stage hierarchy: (1) a shallow LayoutDiT ($\approx 7$M params,
6 layers) generates $z_s$ from noise; (2) a deeper GeometryDiT ($\approx 55$M
params, 12 layers) generates $z_g$ conditioned on $z_s$ via cross-attention.
The frozen Stage-1 decoder then produces the Gaussians.

For \emph{scene completion}, observed latent tokens are held fixed while
unobserved tokens are denoised, conditioned on the layout code derived from
the partial observation.

\paragraph{Stage-1 candidate selection.}
The Stage-2 generator requires a well-converged, well-regularised Stage-1
latent. The R1 checkpoint (local encoder, token-local decoder, Hilbert
ordering) is selected as the Stage-2 input because: (a) it shows the best
generalisation gap in the ablation, and (b) its local-token structure means
$z_s$ and $z_g$ encode their respective spatial regions consistently across
scenes. The LayoutDiT is trained first, then the GeometryDiT conditioned on
frozen LayoutDiT output.

\section{Experimental Configurations}
\label{sec:method_configs}

All runs share the following base configuration: transformer width 384,
12 attention heads, 6 encoder layers, 12 decoder layers, $T=512$ tokens,
$d=32$ per token, 8-frequency Fourier embeddings. Training uses AdamW
($\beta_1=0.9$, $\beta_2=0.999$, weight decay $10^{-2}$), base learning
rate $10^{-4}$, cosine decay with warm restarts ($T_0=800$ epochs),
300-step warmup. The KL weight $\beta=10^{-5}$, bf16 mixed precision,
4 H100 GPUs with DDP. The set loss uses Sinkhorn $\varepsilon=0.05$ with 50
iterations, and the covariance shape weight is $w_\text{cov}=5.0$.
