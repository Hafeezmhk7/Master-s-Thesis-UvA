\section{Motivation}
\label{sec:intro_motivation}

Generating or completing three-dimensional scenes from scratch is one of the more open-ended goals in computer vision and graphics. The downstream applications are wide: rapid content creation for games and film, embodied-AI simulators that need fresh interior layouts, autonomous-driving testing in unseen environments, and robot planning in cluttered spaces. What has changed in the past two years is that the underlying scene representation has matured enough to make this goal look tractable.

3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) \citep{kerbl2023gaussian} has rapidly become the default representation for photorealistic scene reconstruction. A scene is modelled as a set of anisotropic Gaussian splats, each carrying a position, an opacity, a colour (the DC spherical-harmonic coefficient), and an anisotropic covariance parameterised by a rotation and a scale. The representation renders in real time, matches or exceeds the quality of earlier implicit methods such as NeRF \citep{mildenhall2020nerf}, and is explicitly structured: in principle, a network can produce the primitives directly rather than infer them through a coordinate query.

Scene-level 3DGS generation, however, has remained largely unexplored. The standard recipe for generation in adjacent domains is well established. First compress the raw signal into a smooth, compact latent space with a variational autoencoder \citep{kingma2014vae}, then train a generative model on that latent, with diffusion \citep{ho2020ddpm} or its successor flow matching \citep{lipman2023flowmatching, liu2023rectified} now the most common choice and a transformer backbone such as DiT \citep{peebles2023dit} or SiT \citep{ma2024sit} the most common architecture. This recipe is proven at the object level for 3D content \citep{zeng2022lion, zhao2023michelangelo, zhang2024gaussiancube}, but scaling it to whole scenes is a different problem. A scene contains tens of thousands of unordered Gaussian primitives, spans an unbounded volume, mixes dozens of object categories, and comes with no canonical coordinate frame or natural ordering.

Can3Tok \citep{gao2025can3tok} provided the first proof of feasibility: it showed that a scene-level 3DGS variational autoencoder could be trained on a few hundred scenes, and that the resulting latent could be used for generation. This was the architectural starting point of my thesis. The question I wanted to answer was whether the same recipe could be scaled to thousands of heterogeneous indoor scenes, and what would need to change to make that happen. In the early stages of the project the honest answer turned out to be no, not without significant changes. Training the Can3Tok architecture on the SceneSplat-7K dataset \citep{li2025scenesplat} of nearly $8{,}000$ indoor 3DGS scenes failed to converge in any useful sense: validation reconstruction plateaued, and the train-to-test generalisation gap was large. Identifying what caused this, and how to fix it, is what occupied most of the thesis.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{sec:intro_problem}

This thesis studies the construction and training of a semantically aware, scalable, scene-level 3DGS variational autoencoder as Stage~1 of a two-stage generative pipeline for scene generation and completion. The encoder must map a variable-size, unordered Gaussian scene into a fixed-size structured latent $Z \in \bR^{T \times d}$, and the decoder must reconstruct the scene from it. Downstream, a flow-matching Stage~2 model is trained on the resulting latent distribution.

The core difficulty I observed empirically, and which the rest of the thesis is structured around, is that the per-primitive parameters of a \emph{pre-fitted} 3DGS scene are ambiguous in a way that hurts the standard slot-aligned reconstruction objective. Two specific sources of this ambiguity matter.

\paragraph{No canonical order.} A 3DGS scene is fundamentally a set: any permutation of its elements represents the same scene. A decoder that is asked to reconstruct ``the $i$-th Gaussian'' is being asked to reconstruct a target that has no stable identity from one scene to the next. Under an element-wise $\ell_2$ loss between predicted and target Gaussians, slot $i$ in scene $A$ and slot $i$ in scene $B$ might correspond to entirely different parts of the room. Without a canonical ordering, the loss has no persistent signal across scenes and the model never converges to anything compositional.

\paragraph{No fitting-time co-adaptation.} In Can3Tok the splats are jointly optimised alongside the autoencoder, which lets the splat-fitting process subtly co-adapt to whatever tokenisation the network is using. In our setting, the splats are pre-fitted (by the SceneSplat-7K pipeline) and then frozen. The autoencoder receives a fixed set of Gaussians whose within-region arrangement was determined entirely by a rendering-quality optimiser with no knowledge of the downstream tokenisation. The result is that each latent token must compress a much harder, less structured set of primitives than in the original Can3Tok regime.

Both effects become much more severe at scale. At $300$ scenes the model has enough repetition to memorise individual scenes despite these issues. At $3{,}000$ or $6{,}000$ scenes, memorisation is no longer possible, and the issues become the dominant obstacle.

\section{Research Questions}
\label{sec:intro_rqs}

The work addresses one main question and three sub-questions.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Main question.} \textit{What is required to scale a scene-level 3DGS variational autoencoder from a few hundred training scenes to several thousand heterogeneous scenes, and which design choices matter most?}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{RQ1 (Ordering).} Does imposing a canonical spatial order on the Gaussian set restore convergence at scale, and how should that ordering be combined with the decoder structure?

\noindent\textbf{RQ2 (Architecture and generalisation).} Does replacing the global encoder bottleneck and the flat decoder MLP with a spatially local encoder and a shared per-token decoder close the train-to-test generalisation gap that appears at scale?

\noindent\textbf{RQ3 (Semantic structure).} Can per-primitive semantic supervision and a deliberate split between layout and geometry be incorporated into this latent without degrading reconstruction, motivating the design of the Stage~2 generative model?

\section{Contributions}
\label{sec:intro_contributions}

The contributions of this thesis are as follows.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Diagnosis of the scaling failure.} Through a controlled seven-variant ablation, I show that the dominant obstacle to scaling the Can3Tok autoencoder beyond a few hundred scenes is the absence of a stable slot-to-primitive correspondence. Without that, the element-wise $\ell_2$ target is not a learnable function and the validation loss saturates regardless of architecture.

\item \textbf{Fixed-frame Hilbert serialisation as the fix for ordering.} I serialise each scene's Gaussians along a 3D Hilbert space-filling curve \citep{skilling2004hilbert} computed in a fixed canonical frame of radius $10$\,m. This is the same locality-preserving idea that Point Transformer V3 \citep{wu2024ptv3} uses for point cloud tokenisation, but applied here directly to the reconstruction target. Because the frame is fixed (not scene-dependent), slot $i$ corresponds to roughly the same physical region across scenes.

\item \textbf{Anchor-relative position decoding.} Inspired by Scaffold-GS \citep{lu2024scaffold} and the structured prediction in GaussianCube \citep{zhang2024gaussiancube}, I decompose each primitive's predicted position as $\hat{\vmu}_i = a_{\tau(i)} + \rho\,\tanh(o_i)$, where $a_{\tau(i)}$ is the spatial anchor of the token owning Gaussian $i$, $o_i$ is a raw offset predicted per-Gaussian, and $\rho$ is a learnable global scale. The bounded $\tanh$ forces the offset to be a genuine local displacement rather than an absolute position in disguise. Anchors are taken from Hilbert-block centroids and so are exactly reproducible from index alone, which makes them Stage-2 feasible.

\item \textbf{Local encoder plus token-local decoder.} I replace the globally-mixed encoder bottleneck with a windowed cross-attention encoder, in which each geometry query attends only to a $\pm \omega$ window of Hilbert blocks. I pair this with a shared per-token decoder MLP of about $1.6$M parameters, which replaces the flat $\sim 777$M-parameter decoder of Can3Tok. The two changes together close almost all of the train-to-test generalisation gap.

\item \textbf{Small-scale semantic study.} On $300$ scenes, I explore three latent-structure variants and three variants of per-Gaussian InfoNCE supervision \citep{oord2018cpc}. The results motivate the schema-aware Stage~2 design used in the rest of the thesis.

\item \textbf{Schema-aware Stage~2 generator.} Because the seven Stage~1 variants produce latents of two structurally different kinds (a single 512-token latent in five of the seven, a separate 16-token layout latent plus a 512-token geometry latent in the other two), Stage~2 has to be schema-aware. I implement and train a hierarchical flow-matching pipeline \citep{lipman2023flowmatching, peebles2023dit, ma2024sit} with a single \texttt{SceneDiT} for the flat case, a \texttt{LayoutDiT}~+~\texttt{GeometryDiT512} pair for the split case, and corresponding unconditional and conditional \texttt{CompletionDiT} variants for scene completion. Six Stage~2 jobs covering generation and completion on V5, V6, and V7 are reported in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

\item \textbf{Honest characterisation of the residual quality gap.} I identify a residual position-compression issue in the Stage~1 decoder output that propagates to Stage~2, characterise its source, document what I tried to mitigate it, and explain how I would address it in a follow-up. The thesis demonstrates that the architectural directions are correct for the dataset shift from Can3Tok's joint regime to the pre-fitted SceneSplat-7K regime; it does not claim photorealistic reconstruction at the end.

\item \textbf{Code release.} The full codebase, training scripts, and SLURM job files are available at \repo.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Thesis Structure}
\label{sec:intro_structure}

Chapter~\ref{chap:related} places this work in the literature on 3DGS representations, latent generative models, set learning, and semantic representations. Chapter~\ref{chap:method} presents the Stage~1 method in detail, going through the data pipeline, the encoder, the reconstruction objective, the decoder, the local encoder change, the small-scale semantic experiments, and the Stage~2 generator design. Chapter~\ref{chap:results} reports the empirical findings: the 300-scene exploration first, then the seven-experiment large-scale ablation, then the qualitative reconstructions, the per-Gaussian PCA visualisations, and the Stage~2 results. Chapter~\ref{chap:discussion} reflects on what the results support, on the frozen-splat regime, on the position-compression issue, and on the limitations of the work. Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusion} returns to the three research questions. The appendix records the configurations for reproducibility.
