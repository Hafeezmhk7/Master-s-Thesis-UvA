\chapter{Results}
\label{chap:results}

We present results in three groups. \S\ref{sec:results_300} covers the
300-scene exploratory experiments where the model converges well and exact
numbers are available from completed runs. \S\ref{sec:results_large} covers
the large-scale ablation of the four configurations in Table~\ref{tab:configs};
these experiments are currently in training and tables carry \emph{[pending]}
markers where final numbers will be filled in. \S\ref{sec:results_semantic}
covers the qualitative semantic structure.

\noindent\emph{Note on evaluation metrics.} Reconstruction errors are reported
as raw $\ell_2$ norms per batch (the training convention), averaged over
validation batches. Per-attribute errors (Pos, Col, Opa, Scl, Rot) use the
same convention. The generalisation gap is the ratio of held-out error to
training error; a ratio near 1.0 indicates no memorisation.

\subsection{Latent Structure Ablation}
\label{sec:results_300_ablation}

Table~\ref{tab:disent} reports the four evaluation metrics across nine
latent-structure variants trained at 300 scenes. These experiments were
the basis for selecting the Stage-2 design.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption[300-scene latent structure ablation]{%
    \textbf{Latent structure ablation at 300~scenes.}
    \textbf{Ratio$_{\text{lay}}$}: ratio of inter-category to intra-category cosine
    distance in the layout subspace (higher = better semantic clustering).
    \textbf{\%P1}: geometry $\ell_2$ error after cross-scene $z_s$ swap, as a
    fraction of the cross-scene baseline (lower = better disentanglement;
    0\% means swapping $z_s$ across scenes does not disrupt geometry at all).
    \textbf{Stab.@40\%}: cosine similarity of the layout code derived from a 40\%
    partial scan vs.\ the full scan (higher = more stable for completion).
    \textbf{Valid\%}: fraction of $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ prior samples decoding
    to valid geometry.
  }
  \label{tab:disent}
  {\small
  \begin{tabular}{lcccc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Variant} &
    \textbf{Ratio$_{\text{lay}}$}\,$\uparrow$ &
    \textbf{\%P1}\,$\downarrow$ &
    \textbf{Stab.@40\%}\,$\uparrow$ &
    \textbf{Valid\%}\,$\uparrow$ \\
    \midrule
    (C) Baseline                       & 1.048 &  6.5\% & $-$0.05 & 100\% \\
    (A) Disentangled                   & 0.985 &  0.27\% &   0.257 &  94\% \\
    (A) + structured token split       & 0.968 &  0.23\% &   0.204 &  81\% \\
    (A) + LayNCE                       & 0.967 &  0.14\% &   0.249 &  95\% \\
    (A) + PoolNCE                      & 1.027 &  0.18\% &   0.278 &  91\% \\
    (A) + pgNCE                        & 1.036 &  4.5\%  &   0.272 &  86\% \\
    (A) + Pool+pgNCE                   & 0.979 & \textbf{0.08\%} & 0.365 & 95\% \\
    \textbf{(B1) Cross-attn cond.}     & \textbf{1.240} & 0.34\% & \textbf{0.761} & 97\% \\
    (B2) Additive bias                 & 1.046 & 97\%   & $-$0.06 & 99.5\%\\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}}
\end{table*}

Several observations follow from Table~\ref{tab:disent}.

\textbf{Disentanglement works.} Strategy~A (disentangled) reduces the geometry
disruption under cross-scene $z_s$ swap from 6.5\% to 0.27\%, showing that
the separation between layout and geometry subspaces is real, not superficial.
Adding Pool+pgNCE further reduces this to 0.08\%, an $\approx 80\times$
improvement over the baseline.

\textbf{B1 is best for completion readiness.} Strategy~B1 achieves a
Stab.@40\% score of 0.761, far above any variant of Strategy~A (best: 0.365).
This is because B1 uses a deterministic layout projection (no sampling noise),
so the same partial scene always produces the same layout code. B1 is therefore
the natural candidate for the scene completion pipeline.

\textbf{Additive bias fails.} Strategy~B2 collapses to \%P1$=97\%$, indicating
that the layout conditioning did not separate at all. The additive bias
is absorbed by the decoder as a constant offset and provides no
useful conditioning.

\textbf{Key insight for Stage-2 design.} The 300-scene results confirm that a
hierarchical latent split ($z_s$ for layout, $z_g$ for geometry) is achievable
and that the layout code is stable under partial observation. This directly
motivates the two-stage generator described in \S\ref{sec:method_stage2}.
Although the large-scale runs do not include disentanglement (to keep the
ablation focused on the scaling problem), Run~R3 is planned with the
full Strategy~A objectives.

The features cluster meaningfully (positive Fisher ratio, $\approx\!26\%$
accuracy over 72 categories versus 1.4\% random baseline), though not
perfectly (negative silhouette). The modest numbers reflect that the model
is trained primarily for \emph{reconstruction}, not classification; the
contrastive objective is a soft secondary signal.

    R1 (local enc., token dec.)   & -- & -- & $\approx 1\times$ \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \emph{[The qualitative range for R2 ($5$--$8\times$) is observed
  from partially-trained checkpoints. Exact numbers and R1's gap will
  be filled from final checkpoints.]}
\end{table}

The large generalisation gap of R2 ($5$--$8\times$) is a signature of
memorisation. The model has learned scene-specific codes that do not
transfer. With the local encoder, each token encodes a local block of
Gaussians rather than a global scene summary, and similar local patches
(flat floors, walls, corners) activate similar tokens across different
scenes. This compositionality is what closes the gap.

\section{Semantic Structure Visualisation}
\label{sec:results_semantic}

The per-primitive InfoNCE features from the 300-scene experiments are
visualised in Figure~\ref{fig:pca} by projecting them to three principal
components and encoding those as RGB colour.

\begin{figure*}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.97\linewidth, height=0.24\textheight]{example-image}
  \caption[PCA semantic feature visualisation]{%
    \textbf{Per-primitive semantic feature structure (300 scenes, Pool+pgNCE).}
    Each Gaussian's InfoNCE feature is projected to three principal components
    (shown as RGB). Coherent spatial regions correspond to semantic categories:
    floor (one colour family), walls (another), furniture objects (distinct clusters).
    Left column: ground-truth colour. Right column: PCA feature colour.
    %
    \emph{Insert your figures:} render the PLY files from
    \texttt{pca\_visualisations/epoch\_NNN/scene*\_semantic\_infonce.ply}
    in SuperSplat. Take screenshots and place them in a $3\times2$ grid
    (three scenes, ground truth vs.\ PCA colour).
    Export from Canva or Figma as PDF.
  }
  \label{fig:pca}

The visualisations confirm that the contrastive objective produces
category-discriminative features. Flat surfaces (floor, ceiling) cluster
distinctly from vertical surfaces (walls) and from furniture objects. The
clusters are not perfectly separated---points near object boundaries
transition gradually---but the broad structure is clear. This demonstrates
that a semantically organised latent is achievable even without explicit
segmentation supervision in the reconstruction path.
