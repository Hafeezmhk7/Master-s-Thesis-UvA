\chapter{Results}
\label{chap:results}

This chapter reports the empirical findings. I begin with the 300-scene exploration that motivated the disentangled latent and the per-Gaussian semantic supervision (\S\ref{sec:results_300}), then move to the seven-variant large-scale ablation that is the main quantitative contribution (\S\ref{sec:results_7}). Per-component loss breakdowns and validation convergence are reported in \S\ref{sec:results_components} and \S\ref{sec:results_convergence}. A subsection on Stage~2 (\S\ref{sec:results_stage2}) records what is in progress and what has been observed so far. I write up reconstructions qualitatively in \S\ref{sec:results_quality}.

Throughout this chapter the validation metric is the same element-wise $\ell_2$ residual on the 14-dim Gaussian vector that is used in training (Eq.~\eqref{eq:recon_noscaffold}). Two validation sets are reported, the 100 held-out full scenes ($\text{val}^{\text{full}}$, the primary thesis-target metric) and the 88 held-out chunks ($\text{val}^{\text{chunk}}$, an in-distribution diagnostic). All numbers are read from the model's validation logs; for completed runs I report both the best validation score over training and the final epoch numbers.

\section{300-Scene Exploratory Study}
\label{sec:results_300}

\paragraph{Setup.} I subsampled 300 scenes from the chunked ScanNet split (180 train, 60 val, 60 held-out test for swap diagnostics) and ran a sweep over the three latent-structure strategies described in \S\ref{sec:method_semantic} and the three InfoNCE flavours. All other hyperparameters match the eventual large-scale configuration, except for batch size 16 and 200 epochs.

\paragraph{Reconstruction is stable across variants.} At 300 scenes the network has enough capacity to fit any of the four variants, so the raw reconstruction quality is similar across the sweep. The validation $\ell_2$ at epoch 200 sits in the 35--40 range across all four variants. This is consistent with the failure mode at scale being one of generalisation, not of representational capacity.

\paragraph{Layout-vs-geometry stability.} A more diagnostic metric is the \emph{stability} of $z_s$ under partial-scan completion, defined as the cosine similarity between $z_s$ produced from a full scene and $z_s$ from a 40\%-randomly-masked partial version. A high value (close to 1) indicates the layout component is stable under missing data, which is the property the disentanglement is meant to enforce. Table~\ref{tab:300_disentangle} gives the comparison.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption[300-scene latent disentanglement comparison]{\textbf{Layout stability at 300 scenes.} Higher Stab.@40\% is better. Strategy~B1 is the deterministic-layout variant and gives the most stable $z_s$ under masking; Strategy~A is the VAE-sampled variant; Strategy~B2 (additive bias) failed to disentangle and is shown for completeness; Strategy~C (no disentanglement) has no $z_s$ to measure.}
  \label{tab:300_disentangle}
  {\small
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
                     & Val.\ recon.\ $\ell_2$ ($\downarrow$) & Stab.@40\% ($\uparrow$) \\
    \midrule
    Strategy~A       & 36.1 & 0.62 \\
    Strategy~B1      & 35.9 & \textbf{0.76} \\
    Strategy~B2      & 36.4 & 0.31 \\
    Strategy~C       & 35.7 & --   \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}}
\end{table}

Strategy~B1 wins on stability by a comfortable margin while matching the others on raw reconstruction. The deterministic layout code is what gives it this property: the same partial scene routes through the layout projector to the same code regardless of the random KL noise added downstream. Strategy~B2 confirms that an additive layout bias is absorbed by the decoder as a constant offset and gives no useful conditioning.

\paragraph{Per-Gaussian InfoNCE separates categories.} I use a swap diagnostic: take a held-out test scene, generate its $z_s$, replace the bottom-$p$ per cent of Gaussians by category-shuffled samples drawn from other scenes, and measure how many of the swapped Gaussians are still classified as the original category by a simple kNN over the per-Gaussian feature. Higher is better, since it means the swap is invisible at the feature level. Table~\ref{tab:300_nce} shows that combining PoolNCE on $z_s$ with pgNCE on per-Gaussian features (Pool+pgNCE) is the strongest combination at the same reconstruction cost.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \caption[300-scene InfoNCE comparison]{\textbf{Per-Gaussian feature quality at 300 scenes.} Cross-scene swap test (lower P1-of-swap means category-aware features). Pool+pgNCE reduces the swap from 6.5\% to 0.08\% while matching reconstruction; pgNCE alone gives an intermediate value.}
  \label{tab:300_nce}
  {\small
  \begin{tabular}{lcc}
    \toprule
                       & Val.\ recon.\ $\ell_2$ ($\downarrow$) & \%P1 swap ($\downarrow$) \\
    \midrule
    No NCE             & 35.7 & 6.50 \\
    pgNCE              & 35.8 & 0.41 \\
    PoolNCE            & 35.9 & 1.20 \\
    Pool+pgNCE         & 35.9 & \textbf{0.08} \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}}
\end{table}

Variants 6 and 7 in the large-scale ablation use Strategy~B1's deterministic layout idea, together with the layout-vs-geometry KL split described in \S\ref{sec:method_disentangle} and Pool+pgNCE-style supervision. None of these is the literal small-scale code; they are the same ideas scaled up. The takeaway from the 300-scene study is that a layout/geometry split is a viable structural choice and per-Gaussian semantic supervision adds category structure without degrading reconstruction. Both findings carry over.

\section{Seven-Variant Large-Scale Ablation}
\label{sec:results_7}

This section is the main quantitative result of the thesis. The seven configurations of Table~\ref{tab:configs} are trained on the same SceneSplat-7K-derived corpus described in \S\ref{sec:method_dataset}. Validation is on the 100 held-out full scenes and the 88 held-out chunks. All numbers below are read from the SLURM job logs.

\paragraph{Headline numbers.} Table~\ref{tab:7_summary} summarises the best validation reconstruction across training and the corresponding epoch. Two patterns are immediately visible: adding Hilbert reorder plus the position scaffold (V1 $\to$ V2 $\to$ V3) roughly halves the validation $\ell_2$, and the token-local decoder reduces the train-test gap by an even larger factor when paired with the local encoder.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption[Headline numbers for the seven-variant ablation]{\textbf{Best validation reconstruction across the seven Stage~1 variants.} Lower is better. Best epoch indicates when the minimum was observed. ``Final'' columns report the last logged epoch's metrics. Variants 6 and 7 were still training at the time of submission, so for them I report the best value to date.}
  \label{tab:7_summary}
  {\small
  \begin{tabular}{lcccccc}
    \toprule
        & Best $\text{val}^{\text{full}}$ $\ell_2$ ($\downarrow$) & Best epoch & Best $\text{val}^{\text{chunk}}$ $\ell_2$ ($\downarrow$) & Final $\text{val}^{\text{full}}$ & Final $\text{val}^{\text{chunk}}$ & Status \\
    \midrule
    V1  & 98.75  & 60  & 85.86 & 107.79 & 91.70 & finished \\
    V2  & 76.74  & 70  & 80.85 & 81.71  & 75.42 & finished \\
    V3  & 44.63  & 90  & 49.20 & 47.07  & 46.75 & finished \\
    V4  & 98.53  & 40  & 85.58 & 107.79 & 94.11 & finished \\
    V5  & 79.11  & 410 & 84.16 & 79.97  & 84.43 & ckpt @ ep 410 \\

    V7  & 48.89  & 230 & 53.40 & --     & --    & in progress \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}}
\end{table*}

\paragraph{What each row tells us.} I read Table~\ref{tab:7_summary} in three slices.

\emph{Slice 1: ordering alone.} V1 (no ordering, flat decoder) is the un-modified Can3Tok baseline. Validation $\ell_2$ plateaus around 99 with no further improvement after epoch 60. V2 adds the Hilbert reorder; the same architecture, same parameter count, same training schedule, now reaches 77, a 22-point absolute drop. This is the cleanest single-knob result in the thesis: imposing a canonical order on the target makes the same network learn substantially better.

\emph{Slice 2: ordering plus position scaffold.} V3 adds anchor-relative position decoding on top of Hilbert ordering. Validation drops from 77 to 45, almost another factor of two. The position component of the loss does most of the heavy lifting (\S\ref{sec:results_components}); the scaffold gives the decoder a meaningful local origin to predict against.

\emph{Slice 3: architectural change.} V4 isolates the token-local decoder on top of the baseline (no Hilbert reorder, no scaffold, no local encoder). It does not help by itself; validation lands at 99, essentially the same as V1. The mistake here would be to conclude that the token-local decoder is useless. V5 adds the local encoder and the structured latent in step. Validation now drops to 79, very close to V2 (which used a 777M-parameter flat decoder), but at $\sim 1.6$M decoder parameters, a $\sim 500\times$ reduction. The token-local decoder needs the local encoder to feed it the right kind of context: with a global encoder, every token is a global mixture and the per-token MLP has no spatially-meaningful target. The two components are interlocking.

\emph{Slice 4: the full system.} V6 and V7 combine all of the above with the disentangled latent and per-Gaussian InfoNCE. V6 reaches 47.5 (the position scaffold is on), V7 reaches 48.9 (the position scaffold is off). The two are still training at the time of writing, but the trajectories show that V6's validation curve continues to fall after epoch 200, suggesting more room. The differences between V3 and V6 are small in absolute terms, but the V6 latent is split into layout and geometry, which is the substrate the Stage~2 hierarchical generator needs.

\section{Per-Component Loss Breakdown}
\label{sec:results_components}

The aggregate $\ell_2$ in Table~\ref{tab:7_summary} hides where the improvement comes from. Table~\ref{tab:components} gives the per-attribute breakdown at the best validation epoch of each finished or sufficiently advanced run.

\begin{table*}[t]
  \centering
  \caption[Per-attribute loss breakdown]{\textbf{Per-attribute validation loss on the 100 held-out full scenes.} Position ($\vmu$), colour ($c$), opacity ($\alpha$), scale ($s$), and quaternion ($q$). All components are element-wise $\ell_2$, summed across the per-attribute dimension. The position component is by far the dominant one in the early runs and is what the Hilbert order and anchor scaffold target. Opacity and quaternion are nearly saturated even in V1 (the geometry is dense and the orientation has limited variation in indoor scenes), so the absolute improvements there are small.}
  \label{tab:components}
  {\small
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
        & $\vmu$ ($\downarrow$) & $c$ ($\downarrow$) & $\alpha$ ($\downarrow$) & $s$ ($\downarrow$) & $q$ ($\downarrow$) \\
    \midrule
    V1  & 88.04 & 4.21 & 0.81 & 4.32 & 1.37 \\
    V2  & 66.10 & 4.10 & 0.80 & 4.30 & 1.44 \\
    V3  & 34.05 & 4.18 & 0.78 & 4.25 & 1.37 \\
    V4  & 87.79 & 4.31 & 0.80 & 4.31 & 1.32 \\
    V5  & 68.31 & 4.05 & 0.79 & 4.27 & 1.69 \\
    V6  & 36.78 & 4.20 & 0.78 & 4.31 & 1.41 \\
    V7  & 38.10 & 4.22 & 0.78 & 4.31 & 1.48 \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}}
\end{table*}

Position is the dominant component, and it is also the component most directly targeted by the Hilbert reorder and the anchor scaffold. The drop from 88 (V1) to 34 (V3) on the position component matches a similarly steep drop in the aggregate. Colour, opacity, scale, and quaternion are nearly constant across variants. Two consequences:
\begin{itemize}
\item The hard part of scaling Stage~1 is the position prediction. Everything else is roughly solved at all scales the dataset reaches.
\item The DC/AC colour split (\S\ref{sec:method_colour}) is doing its job. Even in V1, where the rest of the system fails, colour reconstruction is already nearly saturated.
\end{itemize}

\section{Stage~2 Generative Results}
\label{sec:results_stage2}

I include this section honestly. At the time of submission, three Stage~2 jobs were running: \texttt{exp5\_completion} on the V5 latent (flat structured, completion task), \texttt{exp6\_layout+geometry+completion} on the V6 latent (split schema, generation and completion), and \texttt{exp7\_layout+geometry+completion} on the V7 latent (split schema, no position scaffold). The flow-matching loss curves are within expected ranges. End-to-end qualitative samples and quantitative metrics on generated scenes were not ready at the time of writing.

\paragraph{What is in place and verified.} The schema-aware bridge between Stage~1 and Stage~2 (\S\ref{sec:method_stage2}) was tested on both flat (V5) and split (V6, V7) checkpoints. Latents load with the correct shapes ($[B, 512, 32]$ for flat structured, $[B, 16, 32]$ plus $[B, 512, 32]$ for split), and a one-step forward through the frozen Stage~1 decoder produces sane reconstructions before any Stage~2 noise is applied. The completion mask logic correctly preserves observed tokens (no leakage), and the rectified-flow velocity targets match the analytic formula $Z_1 - Z_0$ to numerical precision.

\paragraph{What is pending.} The end-to-end samples, the rendering-quality metrics (PSNR, SSIM, LPIPS) of the rendered generated scenes, and the masked-completion qualitative figures. These are listed in the future-work section of Chapter~\ref{chap:conclusion} and constitute the next concrete milestones for the project.
