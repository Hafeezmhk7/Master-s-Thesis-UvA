\section{What the Numbers Are, and How to Read Them}
\label{sec:res_status}
% =====================================================================

The work has two experimental phases, and we are explicit about which is which so that the reader does not over-read the smaller one. The first is a \emph{small-scale exploration} on a few hundred scenes, which we used to make design decisions cheaply: the sampling budget, the serialisation order, the colour decomposition, the latent-structure variants, and the Stage~2 positional encoding were all chosen here. Numbers from this phase are exploratory, sometimes from a single run, and we report them only to show how a decision was reached; they are flagged as such. The second is the \emph{large-scale} phase, in which a fixed configuration is trained on roughly $6{,}700$ heterogeneous scenes (\autoref{sec:method_setup}), and which carries the central reconstruction claims of the thesis.

Two measurement caveats apply throughout and we state them once. First, the aggregate reconstruction loss is a weighted sum whose weights and auxiliary terms differ slightly between latent-structure variants, so the aggregate is \emph{not} directly comparable across configurations; we therefore lead with the per-attribute errors, which are on the same raw scale for every run, and in particular with the position error, which is the cleanest and most interpretable channel. Second, the orientation channel cannot be read from the raw quaternion error at all, because that error is blind to the orientation gauge of \autoref{sec:method_overview}; we report orientation only through a gauge-invariant covariance error and treat the raw quaternion number as uninformative. These two points are not incidental: they are consequences of the identifiability analysis, and they decide which columns in the tables below mean something.

\section{Scaling and Reconstruction (RQ1)}
\label{sec:res_scaling}
% =====================================================================

The starting observation, which motivates everything that follows, is the one the small-scale exploration surfaced and the large-scale phase confirmed: the inherited encoder and decoder reconstruct a few hundred scenes well, but the same configuration, trained without the structural changes of \autoref{sec:methodology}, does not reach a comparable reconstruction once the training set is scaled to several thousand heterogeneous scenes. The position error in particular stays an order of magnitude above where it eventually settles. The rest of this section reports what closed that gap.

\paragraph{The architecture ladder.} \autoref{tab:ladder} reports the per-attribute reconstruction error on the held-out full scenes for a ladder of configurations, each adding one structural change to the one above it. The reduction in position error is the headline. The global baseline, with the inherited global-bottleneck encoder and the flat decoder, sits at a position error above five thousand. Adding the token-local decoder \emph{on its own} changes essentially nothing, which is itself informative and we return to it in \autoref{sec:res_identifiability}. Replacing the global bottleneck with the windowed per-token encoder roughly halves the position error. The two changes that move it by an order of magnitude are the anchor-relative position decoding of \autoref{eq:anchor} and the structured per-token (disentangled) latent, and combining them gives the lowest error we obtained.

\paragraph{Two independent routes to a low position error.} The most useful thing the ladder shows is that there are \emph{two} different changes that each cut the position error by roughly an order of magnitude, and they are not the same mechanism. The first is the anchor-relative decoding: applied to the otherwise unchanged global baseline, it drops the position error from above five thousand to about five hundred and forty. The second is the structured per-token latent (the disentangled variant), which reaches a comparable position error of about five hundred and forty \emph{without} an explicit anchor, through the one-to-one correspondence between latent tokens and Hilbert blocks. Each addresses the within-region placement gauge of \autoref{sec:method_overview}, one by forcing the coarse position into a per-block anchor and the other by giving each block its own token, and using both together lowers the error a little further, to about four hundred and fifty. We read this convergence of two independent interventions on the same value as evidence that what is being removed is a real, shared quantity (the placement gauge) rather than an artefact of one particular trick.

\paragraph{A position-error floor.} Across the structured configurations the position error does not fall below roughly four hundred and fifty, even as opacity and scale continue to improve and training continues. We interpret this floor as the residual within-block placement gauge that a parameter-space objective cannot remove: once a token is responsible for a block of primitives, exactly where each primitive sits inside that block is not determined by the rendered scene, so an $L_2$ target on absolute positions is being asked to fit nuisance. This is consistent with the identifiability thesis and is, we think, the clearest single piece of evidence for it (\autoref{sec:discussion}).

\paragraph{Generalisation.} \autoref{tab:gen} reports, for each configuration, the error on held-out full scenes against the error on held-out chunks. We use this pair as a generalisation check, but with an important caveat that we did not appreciate at first: full scenes are intrinsically harder to reconstruct than chunks, because they contain more primitives over a larger extent, so a ratio slightly above one is expected even with no overfitting and the ratio conflates difficulty with generalisation. Read with that caveat, the numbers are reassuring rather than diagnostic: every configuration, including the global baseline, generalises with a full-to-chunk ratio between about $1.0$ and $1.2$, and the converged models show validation error close to their training error, so overfitting is not the bottleneck at this data scale. This is a correction to a hypothesis we held from the small-scale phase, where a global encoder on a few hundred scenes did show a much larger train-to-test gap; at scale, the volume of data controls overfitting, and the benefit of the local encoder shows up as better absolute reconstruction (\autoref{tab:ladder}) rather than as a closed generalisation gap. We flag this explicitly because it changes the claim we can make for the local encoder.

\section{Identifiability, Ordering, Sampling, and Locality (RQ2)}
\label{sec:res_identifiability}
% =====================================================================

This section isolates the factors behind the scaling behaviour. Two of them, the sampling budget and the serialisation order, were settled in the small-scale phase and we report them as such; the others are read off the large-scale ladder of \autoref{tab:ladder}.

\paragraph{Sampling budget (small-scale).} With pre-fitted and frozen splats, we found that sampling a smaller budget of primitives per scene (on the order of $10$k) converged more cleanly than a larger budget (on the order of $40$k), which is the opposite of the behaviour reported for the original setting in which the splats are jointly optimised while they are sampled \citep{gao2025can3tok}. Our reading follows directly from the placement gauge: the latent has a fixed token count, so a larger budget packs more primitives into each token's block (roughly $79$ per token at $40$k against $20$ at $10$k for a $512$-token latent), and the extra primitives are exactly the within-block arrangement that is least identifiable. In the original pipeline the fit can co-adapt the primitives to the sampled target; with frozen splats it cannot, so the larger target is simply more nuisance per token. \autoref{tab:sampling} records the comparison. We note that our large-scale runs nonetheless used the larger budget for continuity with the base pipeline, which is a limitation we return to in \autoref{sec:discussion}.

\paragraph{Serialisation order (small-scale).} Replacing the opacity rank with a fixed-frame space-filling-curve order made the element-wise target spatially stable and was, in our experience, the single change that most improved convergence early on, consistent with the locality argument for the Hilbert curve over the Z-order curve \citep{wu2024ptv3}. We are careful not to overstate it: ordering made the slot-aligned target learnable and improved convergence, but on its own it did not bring reconstruction to the level the structural changes later reached, so we treat it as necessary but not sufficient. \autoref{tab:ordering} records the order comparison.

\paragraph{The token-local decoder is not sufficient on its own.} The second row of \autoref{tab:ladder} is a deliberately included null result. Swapping the flat decoder for the shared per-token decoder, with no change to the global encoder and no anchor decoding, leaves the position error essentially unchanged (about $5372$ against $5386$). A smaller, better-structured decoder does not help if the latent it reads from is still a globally mixed code and the position target is still an absolute coordinate. The decoder change only pays off once it is paired with the local encoder, which gives each token a local receptive field, and with the anchor decoding, which changes what the position target is. We report the null result because it rules out the simplest explanation (that the win is just a better decoder) and points at the encoder and the target instead.

\paragraph{Orientation: the covariance is learned, the quaternion is not.} Under the gauge-invariant covariance objective, scale and the full covariance of the Gaussians are learnable and do improve over training, while the raw element-wise quaternion error barely moves. This is exactly what the orientation-gauge analysis predicts and is not a failure of the objective: the covariance $\Sigma = R\,\mathrm{diag}(s^2)\,R^\top$ is identified by the data and is reconstructed, but the quaternion that parameterises its orientation is identified only up to the double cover, axis relabelling, and, near isotropy, almost any rotation, so the raw quaternion error is measuring gauge noise. \autoref{tab:objective} reports the covariance error and colour fidelity under the element-wise, covariance, and set objectives. We report orientation \emph{only} through the covariance error for this reason, and we recommend reading the rotation column of \autoref{tab:ladder} as ``gauge-locked'' rather than as a quality number.

\paragraph{Per-primitive semantic clustering.} The per-primitive contrastive objective organises the per-primitive feature space so that primitives of the same category group together. \autoref{fig:pca} shows principal-component projections of these features, in which object regions appear as coherently coloured clusters. This is the qualitative evidence that the latent carries semantic structure and not only geometry. The same structure is visible in the projection of the disentangled semantic tokens $z_s$ of the two structured models we carry to Stage~2, which is what first told us those two checkpoints, and not the geometric-only ones, had a usable layout subspace.

\paragraph{Colour through the global shape embedding.} Decoding colour as a residual about a per-scene mean predicted from the global shape embedding (the DC/AC split of \autoref{sec:method_decoder}) improved colour fidelity in the exploration by separating the coarse colour statistic, carried globally, from the fine per-primitive variation, decoded locally. The large-scale numbers are consistent with this: the configurations that use the structured local path with the mean-colour head (the lower three rows of \autoref{tab:ladder}) reach a colour error around $571$, against around $850$ to $870$ for the global-bottleneck configurations, a gap we attribute partly to the colour decomposition and partly to the local receptive field. This is reflected in the colour column of \autoref{tab:objective}.

\paragraph{Disentanglement (small-scale).} In the small-scale phase we measured the effect of the disentangled latent and its objectives on category clustering in the layout subspace, on the degree to which swapping the layout subspace across scenes leaves geometry intact (a measure of disentanglement), and on the stability of the layout code under partial observation (a proxy for completion readiness). \autoref{tab:disentangle} reproduces those exploratory results. They indicate that the disentanglement objectives produced a real separation, since swapping the layout subspace disrupted geometry much less than a cross-scene baseline, and that a deterministic layout code was the most stable under partial observation, which is the property a completion model needs. These numbers are exploratory and were the basis for design decisions, not a final measurement.

\section{Choosing Stage 1 Representations for Stage 2 (RQ4, part 1)}
\label{sec:res_selection}
% =====================================================================

Before training any Stage~2 model we used the Stage~1 results to decide which representations were worth the second-stage cost, since training a full generation and completion pipeline for every Stage~1 variant was not feasible. This selection is itself a result, because it follows directly from what the latents can and cannot support, and we describe the reasoning rather than presenting it as a foregone choice.

Two requirements drove the decision. Generation in our design is hierarchical, a layout model followed by a geometry model (\autoref{sec:method_stage2}), so it needs a latent with a separate layout subspace $z_s$. Completion is posed as latent inpainting over spatial regions, so it needs a latent whose tokens correspond to spatial regions that can be individually masked. A latent that is a single globally mixed code satisfies neither cleanly: masking one of its dimensions hides an arbitrary latent direction rather than a region, and there is no layout subspace to condition on.

\autoref{tab:selection} maps the Stage~1 variants to what they support. The two disentangled models are the only ones with both a separate $z_s$ and a per-token spatial geometry latent, so they support generation and completion; they differ only in the anchor decoding, which makes the pair a clean ablation of that choice carried into Stage~2. The structured-but-flat model (the local encoder without the disentangled split) has spatial tokens but no $z_s$, so it supports completion without layout conditioning, which we keep precisely so that completion can be compared with and without the semantic conditioning. The global models, including the best-reconstructing one with anchor decoding, have neither a $z_s$ nor per-token spatial structure, so although that model reconstructs best in \autoref{tab:ladder}, its latent is the least suited to the second stage; we note this tension openly rather than letting the best Stage~1 number dictate the Stage~2 choice. The outcome is a small set of second-stage runs: hierarchical generation and completion on the two disentangled models, and completion without conditioning on the structured-flat model.

\section{Generation and Completion (RQ4, part 2)}
\label{sec:res_stage2}
% =====================================================================

The Stage~2 models of \autoref{sec:method_stage2} are implemented and trained on the selected representations, with the frozen Stage~1 encoder and decoder. At the time of writing the large-scale Stage~2 runs were finishing and their quantitative evaluation was in progress, so we describe the setup, report the one Stage~2 ablation that is already complete, and leave marked space for the final results. We are deliberately conservative in what we claim here.

\paragraph{Positional encoding for the latent (small-scale).} The one Stage~2 design choice we settled before the large runs is how to encode token position in the latent flow-matching transformers. We compared a learned absolute position embedding against one-dimensional and three-dimensional rotary embeddings \citep{su2021roformer} on a few hundred scenes, separately for the layout and geometry models. The geometry tokens, which lie along the Hilbert curve and therefore carry spatial order, were best served by the one-dimensional rotary embedding, with the three-dimensional variant second and the learned absolute embedding third; our reading is that the Hilbert ordering already linearises spatial locality, so a rotary embedding over the sequence index recovers most of the neighbourhood structure, while the three-dimensional variant has to rely on a fixed scaffold lattice that is less informative at generation time when positions are not yet known. The layout tokens, which are abstract and have no spatial geometry, showed no benefit from a rotary embedding and we use the learned absolute embedding there. We therefore use a one-dimensional rotary embedding for the geometry and completion models and a learned absolute embedding for the layout model. \autoref{tab:rope} records the comparison.
