    \chapter*{Abstract}
        \begin{adjustwidth}{4cm}{4cm}
3D Gaussian Splatting has become a standard representation for photorealistic
scene reconstruction, yet scene-level generation remains largely unexplored.
Compressing an entire scene of unordered Gaussian primitives into a structured
latent space is the prerequisite. This thesis studies a semantically aware,
scalable, scene-level Gaussian-splatting variational autoencoder as Stage~1 of
a two-stage generative pipeline. We show that the central obstacle to scaling
such an autoencoder from a few hundred scenes to several thousand is
\emph{identifiability}: pre-fitted Gaussian scenes contain large amounts of
gauge freedom in the ordering of the primitive set, in the quaternion
parameterisation of orientation, and in the within-region placement of
individual primitives, none of which a naive reconstruction objective can
learn. We further identify that the sampling budget interacts non-trivially
with this gauge: with frozen, pre-fitted splats a smaller budget works
significantly better than the large budget used when splats are jointly
optimised, a finding with practical consequences for the field. Our remedies
combine a fixed-frame Hilbert space-filling serialisation that makes the
slot-aligned target learnable, permutation-invariant and gauge-invariant
reconstruction objectives, and a spatially local per-token encoder that yields
a compositional latent. Contrastive and disentanglement objectives add semantic
and layout structure. Ablation experiments confirm that encoding locality is
the single change most responsible for closing the train-to-test generalisation
gap at scale. The second-stage hierarchical flow-matching generator for scene
generation and completion is designed and partially implemented.
        \end{adjustwidth}
